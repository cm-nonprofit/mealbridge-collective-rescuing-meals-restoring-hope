The Problem

Good Food Is Going to Waste While Families Go Hungry

Every day, restaurants discard unsold meals, grocery stores toss perfectly edible produce, and local farms lose surplus harvest — all while one in eight families in our community struggle to put food on the table.

The Disconnect Is Staggering

The infrastructure to move surplus food to those in need simply doesn't exist at the community level. Donors want to help but face logistical barriers. Shelters and food banks run on thin margins. Volunteers are willing but lack coordination.

Our Journey

From a Student Project to a Community Lifeline

What started as a computer science project to optimize food logistics has grown into a movement that feeds thousands each month.

The Spark — A Campus Observation

While working on a computer science assignment about logistics optimization, test test noticed how much food the campus dining hall threw away nightly. The question became: what if technology could redirect that food to people who need it?

The Prototype — A Simple App

test test built a mobile app prototype connecting food donors with volunteer drivers. Within weeks, three local restaurants signed up and volunteers began making same-day pickups to deliver meals to a nearby homeless shelter.

Community Response — Growing Fast

Word spread quickly. Grocery stores joined, local farms offered surplus produce, and families in need began reaching out directly. The project outgrew its academic origins and demanded a formal organization.

MealBridge Collective — Born

Today, MealBridge Collective operates weekly meal distribution drives, manages a growing network of farm partners, and runs the mobile platform that coordinates hundreds of same-day food rescues every month.

Meet the Founder

test test

Founder & Executive Director — Computer Science Student

Growing up, test test saw firsthand how food insecurity affects families and communities. During a computer science course focused on real-world problem solving, the idea struck: what if the same logistics algorithms that power delivery services could redirect wasted food to the people who need it?

That question became MealBridge Collective — an organization that now rescues thousands of pounds of food every month and has built a community-driven network of donors, volunteers, and partners committed to ending hunger, one meal at a time.

"Technology should serve everyone. If an algorithm can optimize a delivery route for profit, it can optimize one for compassion. That's what MealBridge is about — using code to make sure no one goes hungry."
